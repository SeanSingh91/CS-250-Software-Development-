Reflection
How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? 
How does creating "user stories" help with this?

Interpreting user needs starts before any code gets written. During the SNHU Travel project, the 
most important shift I made as a Product Owner was learning to think about the why behind a feature, not 
just the what. Writing each user story in the "As a traveler, I want to… so that I can…" format forced 
that discipline. It is easy to write a feature description. It is harder to trace that feature back to 
something a real user would actually experience and value. User stories help because they keep the 
conversation grounded in outcomes rather than implementation. When I created the Personalized Travel 
Profile story, attaching specific acceptance criteria, a vacation type multi-select field, a departure 
city input, preferred travel months, a progress bar, turned a vague idea into something the team 
could build against and a tester could verify. Without those criteria, the team would have been guessing
at what "done" means.

How do I approach developing programs?
What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

My approach to development starts with questions. During the wellness travel pivot in Module Five, my 
first instinct was to start coding immediately when the direction changed. What I learned was that acting
on incomplete information mid-sprint is one of the fastest ways to create rework. Slowing down to ask 
targeted questions, what is changing, what is staying the same, what does the tester need to 
know, prevented building against requirements that were already outdated. The Agile processes I intend 
to carry forward are Sprint Planning, the Daily Scrum, and the Retrospective. Sprint Planning keeps scope 
visible and prevents the team from taking on more than it can finish. The Daily Scrum surfaces blockers 
early. The Retrospective creates a structured moment to capture lessons before the next sprint 
begins, rather than carrying problems silently into future work.

What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

Being a good team member means treating transparency as a responsibility rather than an option. 
Two moments from this project stand out. The first was the clarification email I wrote as a tester 
before beginning test case development, rather than making assumptions about the layout of the Top 5 
DestinationList, I asked three specific, numbered questions that gave the Product Owner clear things to 
respond to quickly. The second was the developer email during the wellness pivot, where I separated 
questions for the Product Owner from questions for the tester so each person could act on what was 
relevant to their role. Both examples share the same principle: good communication is targeted, timely, and 
action-oriented. On a Scrum team, that kind of communication is not optional, it is what keeps 
transparency, inspection, and adaptation from being abstract ideals.
